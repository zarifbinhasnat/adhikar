# Digital Evidence Admissibility for Mobile-Captured Recordings
## A Legal and Technical Research Note for Adhikar — Know Your Rights BD

*Prepared: May 2026 | Jurisdiction: People's Republic of Bangladesh | Status: Working draft*

---

## Abstract

This note examines the legal and technical requirements for mobile-captured audio, video, and photographic evidence to be admissible in Bangladeshi courts. It surveys the applicable statutory framework — particularly the Evidence (Amendment) Act 2022 — alongside international forensic standards (RFC 3161, ISO/IEC 27037) and existing court-validated implementations such as the IBA's eyeWitness to Atrocities. The note concludes with a gap analysis of the current evidence vault implementation and a prioritised technical roadmap.

---

## 1. The Bangladeshi Statutory Framework

### 1.1 Evidence Act 1872 — as amended 2022

The primary statute governing evidence admissibility in Bangladesh is the **Evidence Act 1872**, substantially updated by the **Evidence (Amendment) Act 2022 (Act No. XX of 2022)**, enacted 3 November 2022. This amendment introduces the most significant modernisation of evidence law since independence.

**Section 3** defines "Documentary Evidence" broadly as "any matter expressed or described upon any substance by means of letters, figures or marks." Courts have consistently interpreted this to encompass digital recordings since *Mrs. Khaleda Akter v. State, 37 DLR (1985) 275*.

**Section 65A** (inserted 2022) — *"Special provisions as to evidence relating to digital records."* Acts as the gateway provision; substantive requirements are in §65B.

**Section 65B** (inserted 2022) — *"Admissibility of Digital Records."* This is the operative section for any evidence captured by a mobile application.

- **§65B(1):** Secondary copies of digital records (printouts, stored data) are admissible without requiring the original, provided conditions are satisfied.
- **§65B(2):** Four conditions for computer-generated output admissibility:
  1. The device was in regular operational use during the relevant period
  2. Information was input consistently during routine activity
  3. The device was operating without malfunction or compromise
  4. The information derives from prior legitimate inputs
- **§65B(4) — Certificate Requirement (mandatory for contested evidence):** A certificate must be produced containing:
  - Identification of the electronic file
  - Description of the creation process
  - Device details (make, model, OS version)
  - Confirmation of §65B(2) conditions
  - Signature of a person holding an "official position" in relation to the device
  - A declaration that the contents are true to the best of knowledge and belief

This certificate is the single most important procedural document in a contested digital evidence case. An application that auto-generates this certificate — pre-populated with cryptographically verified metadata — provides substantial practical value to users who are unlikely to know this requirement exists.

**Section 67A** — Proof of digital signatures.

**Sections 81A, 85A, 85B, 85C** — Presumptions of genuineness for digital records bearing recognised signatures.

**Section 88A** — Digital communications may be presumed genuine in certain circumstances.

### 1.2 ICT Act 2006

**Section 6** — Electronic records are legally valid and cannot be denied effect solely because they exist in electronic form.

**Section 63** — Recording a person's private communications without consent is a punishable offence. This has direct implications for the recording feature: the application must clearly disclose to the user that recording is lawful only when the user is a party to the interaction or the interaction occurs in a public place during an official act (such as a police stop). Failure to display this disclosure creates potential criminal liability for the user.

**Section 87** — Amends the definition of "document" in the Penal Code to include documents generated by electronic means, extending criminal law's document concept to digital evidence.

---

### 1.3 Cyber Security Act 2023 (replacing Digital Security Act 2018)

**Section 58** — Forensic evidence produced or obtained under the Act is explicitly admissible. This section also makes tampering with or manipulating electronic evidence a separate punishable offence (read with §211 of the Penal Code). The repealed DSA 2018's evidence provisions carry forward in substantially similar form.

---

### 1.4 Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

The CrPC as applied in Bangladesh contains no standalone digital evidence provision, representing a structural gap. However:

- **Section 161** — Courts have permitted audio-video recordings of witness examinations to enter the record
- **Section 164(1)** — Confessions before a magistrate may now be audio-video recorded
- **Section 165** — Empowers courts to order production of digital records

---

### 1.5 Speedy Trial Tribunal Act 2002

**Section 16** — Explicitly recognises "video cassettes or audio cassettes and still photographs as admissible evidence." The earliest explicit statutory recognition of audio-visual evidence (predating the Evidence Amendment by 20 years), establishing the judicial comfort upon which the 2022 reforms were built.

---

### 1.6 Anti-Terrorism Act 2009

**Section 21** — Social media evidence and electronic records are admissible in terrorism proceedings, covering screenshots, posts, and electronic communications.

---

### 1.7 Landmark Judicial Decisions

**Mrs. Khaleda Akter v. State, 37 DLR (1985) 275**
The foundational ruling. The Court held that video cassettes "should come within the definition of the document" under §3 of the Evidence Act. Established: audio/video is documentary evidence.

**The State v. Qamrul Islam & Others, 2017 (2) LNJ (HCD) 303**
High Court: "Video record footage is a document within the meaning of the Evidence Act and is accordingly admissible if otherwise relevant."

**Biswajit Murder Case, 70 DLR (2018) HCD 26**
The most practically important ruling. The Court held video footage and photographs admissible *without corroboration by the authors* provided the footage was *"officially handed over to the Investigating Officer under a seizure list."* Principle established: a documented handover authenticates ipso facto.

**Major Bazlul Huda & Others v. The State, 18 BLT (AD) (2010) 7**
The Appellate Division set mandatory requirements for contested digital evidence:
1. The original storage medium must be produced
2. A certificate from the producer must be attached, certifying recording date and place
3. The producer's signature must be verified

This is the pre-2022 equivalent of what became the §65B(4) certificate — showing the judiciary had already converged on this requirement before it was codified.

## 2. International Technical Standards

### 2.1 RFC 3161 — Trusted Timestamping (IETF, August 2001)

RFC 3161, updated by RFC 5816, defines the Internet X.509 PKI Time-Stamp Protocol (TSP). It is the globally dominant standard for proving that specific data existed at a specific point in time, independent of device clock manipulation.

**Protocol operation:**
1. Compute SHA-256 hash of the media file bytes
2. Construct a `TimeStampReq` (DER-encoded) containing the hash, algorithm OID, and a nonce
3. POST to a Time Stamping Authority (TSA) over HTTPS
4. TSA returns a `TimeStampResp` containing a signed `TSTInfo` with: `messageImprint` (the submitted hash), `genTime` (authoritative timestamp), and the TSA's certificate chain
5. Store the DER-encoded token alongside the evidence record
6. Anyone can verify offline using the TSA's public key — no network call required post-issuance

**Legal recognition:**
- **EU eIDAS Regulation Art. 41:** Qualified electronic timestamps cannot be rejected as evidence solely because they are electronic. A qualified timestamp creates a legal presumption that the data existed at the stated time.
- **Bangladesh:** No direct statutory recognition yet, but a TSA-signed token directly satisfies the "creation process description" element of a §65B(4) certificate and has persuasive weight as a third-party verification mechanism.

**Free TSA providers suitable for mobile apps:**
- `freetsa.org` — free, RFC 3161 compliant
- `timestamp.digicert.com` — industry standard
- `tsa.belgium.be` — eIDAS-qualified (EU-recognised)

### 2.2 ISO/IEC 27037:2012 — Digital Evidence Handling Guidelines

*"Information technology — Security techniques — Guidelines for identification, collection, acquisition and preservation of digital evidence."*

This standard defines four processes and three principles governing handling of potential digital evidence. Cited in courts in the UK, Australia, the EU, and increasingly in South Asian jurisdictions.

**Four required processes:**
1. **Identification** — Recognising and documenting potential digital evidence
2. **Collection** — Acquiring evidence while maintaining integrity
3. **Acquisition** — Creating forensically sound copies with hash verification
4. **Preservation** — Maintaining integrity throughout the evidence lifecycle

**Three mandatory principles:**
1. **Auditability** — Every action on evidence must be documented so an independent expert could audit it
2. **Repeatability** — Forensic processes must produce the same result when repeated
3. **Reproducibility** — A different examiner using the same methods must produce the same result

**Chain of custody requirement:** A contemporaneous document must record: acquisition to a specific device, all movements, all subsequent copies made for analysis. The chain must be maintained for the period specified by local jurisdiction after proceedings conclude.

### 2.3 Cryptographic Hash Standards

| Algorithm | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **SHA-256** (NIST FIPS 180-4) | ✅ Universal standard | Minimum for digital evidence; accepted by all major court systems |
| **SHA-3** (NIST FIPS 202) | ✅ Accepted | Alternative mathematical basis (Keccak); not yet commonly required |
| **SHA-1** | ❌ Deprecated | Collision attacks demonstrated 2017; do not use |
| **MD5** | ❌ Deprecated | Collision attacks demonstrated 2008–2012; do not use |

---

### 2.4 Tamper-Evident vs. Tamper-Proof

**Tamper-evident** systems detect and prove tampering occurred after the fact. Example: if a file is modified, recomputing its SHA-256 hash produces a different value, proving modification. Courts require and accept tamper-evident evidence — they do not demand the impossible standard of preventing all tampering; they require a reliable mechanism to detect it.

**Tamper-proof** systems theoretically prevent any modification. Hardware security modules (HSMs) and secure enclaves approach this. Courts do not require tamper-proof evidence.

---

### 2.5 Blockchain and Distributed Ledger Approaches

**OpenTimestamps (OTS):** An open protocol that hashes data, aggregates it into a merkle tree, and submits the merkle root to the Bitcoin blockchain via OP_RETURN transaction. Proves existence at block timestamp (typically 10–60 minutes after submission). Free and open source. Used by ProofMode (planned integration). Accepted by French courts as of March 2025 as corroborating evidence.

**Practical recommendation for Bangladesh context:** An RFC 3161 TSA timestamp is the safer primary anchor — it has a direct path to court admissibility via §65B(4). Blockchain timestamping (OTS, Bernstein.io, Woleet) serves as a compelling supplementary layer rather than the primary mechanism.

## 3. Existing Court-Validated Implementations

### 3.1 eyeWitness to Atrocities (International Bar Association)

Launched June 2015. As of 2025: 53,000+ documented items; dossiers submitted to the ICC; accepted in Ukrainian courts for war crime damage claims (2022–2024).

**Technical mechanisms:**
- SHA-256 hash computed over pixel data **at the moment of capture**, before file write
- GPS, date/time, device sensor data, and surrounding Bluetooth/WiFi network identifiers captured as an environmental fingerprint
- Each item cryptographically signed with IBA-controlled keys
- Separate encrypted gallery inaccessible to standard phone extraction tools
- On upload: new hash computed and compared to original; mismatch = in-transit tampering detected
- Immutable server storage — user cannot modify or delete after upload
- Full access log maintained server-side

### 3.2 ProofMode (Guardian Project / WITNESS)

Open source. Android. Active development.

**Technical mechanisms:**
- SHA-1 and SHA-256 computed over actual media file bytes
- PGP key pair generated at first launch; all proof metadata signed with user's private key
- Proof stored in a **companion file** (same base filename, `.proof` extension) — never modifies the original media file
- Metadata: GPS with accuracy, network state, cell tower data, device make/model
- SafetyNet API check logged (flags emulator execution)
- OpenTimestamps integration planned

**Acknowledged limitation:** No RFC 3161 timestamping; no server-side chain of custody; no §65B(4) certificate infrastructure.

## 4. Gap Analysis — Current Application vs. Court Admissibility

### 4.1 Current evidence pipeline

```
Camera captures → file saved to local URI →
32-bit djb2 hash(URI_string + GPS_string + timestamp_string) →
plaintext JSON object written to AsyncStorage
```

### 4.2 Gap matrix

| Gap | Severity | Legal standard violated |
|---|---|---|
| Hash not computed over file bytes | 🔴 Critical | §45 Evidence Act; SHA-256 minimum |
| No RFC 3161 trusted timestamp | 🔴 Critical | §65B(4) creation process; RFC 3161 |
| No §65B(4) certificate generator | 🔴 Critical | §65B(4) Evidence Act 2022 |
| Storage is plaintext and mutable | 🔴 Critical | ISO/IEC 27037 preservation principle |
| No device fingerprint at capture | 🟠 Significant | §65B(4) device details requirement |
| No chain-of-custody access log | 🟠 Significant | ISO/IEC 27037 auditability principle |
| GPS accuracy/altitude not stored | 🟡 Moderate | ISO/IEC 27037 metadata standard |
| No structured incident report | 🟡 Moderate | Biswajit precedent (corroborating context) |
| No consent disclosure before record | ⚠️ Legal risk | ICT Act 2006, §63 |

### 4.3 Specific findings

**Hash function:** The current implementation applies a custom 32-bit djb2-variant hash to a concatenated metadata *string* — not to the file itself. This hash has high collision probability and proves nothing about file integrity. Under §45 of the Evidence Act, a court-appointed forensic expert would dismiss it.

**Timestamp:** `new Date().toISOString()` reads the device clock, which a user or adversary can set to any value. It provides no independent verification of when a recording was made.

**Storage:** AsyncStorage stores evidence records as plaintext JSON. The records are mutable — a record can be overwritten or deleted without detection. This violates the basic preservation requirement of ISO/IEC 27037 and would undermine chain-of-custody arguments.

**Device metadata:** The §65B(4) certificate requires device make, model, OS version, and app version. None of these are currently captured at recording time.

## 5. Technical Roadmap

### Phase 1 — Minimum viable court-grade (critical gaps)
1. Replace hash → `Crypto.digest(SHA256, fileBytes)` via `expo-crypto`
2. Capture device fingerprint at record time (`expo-device`, `expo-application`)
3. Display consent/public-place disclosure before recording begins
4. Store full GPS object (`accuracy`, `altitude`, `altitudeAccuracy`, `speed`)

### Phase 2 — Trusted timestamp and certificate
5. POST SHA-256 hash to RFC 3161 TSA; store the DER-encoded token
6. Generate pre-populated §65B(4) certificate PDF (`expo-print`) at evidence save time
7. Encrypt evidence metadata at rest (AES-256 via `expo-crypto`)
8. Append-only access log per evidence record, each entry hashing the previous

### Phase 3 — Production and hackathon tier
9. PGP signing with per-user key pair (backend-assisted)
10. Immutable server upload to Supabase Storage with server-side hash re-verification
11. WiFi/Bluetooth environmental fingerprint at capture (corroborating location)
12. Structured bilingual incident report exported as a court-ready PDF dossier
13. OpenTimestamps Bitcoin anchor as secondary immutability layer

## 7. Implementation Directive

The gaps identified in §4 are not aspirational improvements — they are the difference between evidence that survives a §45 forensic challenge and evidence that does not. Any agent or developer extending the evidence vault must treat the following as hard constraints, not optional enhancements: every evidence record must carry a SHA-256 hash computed over actual file bytes, an RFC 3161 timestamp token from a recognised TSA, a device fingerprint (make, model, OS, app version), a full GPS accuracy object, and an append-only access log. The §65B(4) certificate must be auto-generated and exportable as a PDF at the time of save — not retrofitted later. Storage must be encrypted. The consent disclosure must precede every recording session. These requirements derive directly from the Evidence (Amendment) Act 2022 and ISO/IEC 27037; they are the minimum bar for the evidence vault to deliver on the product's core promise. Full implementation specification, architectural decisions, design system rules, and agent-specific coding instructions are maintained in a separate file not tracked by version control — consult it before making any change to the evidence pipeline, the storage layer, or the recording flow.
